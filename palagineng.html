Sergey V. Palagin - Russian military pilot, lieutenant colonel, took part in combat operations during the Ossetian-Ingush conflict (October - November 1992), the First and Second Chechen wars, the antiterrorist operation in Dagestan (August - September 1999) and the armed conflict in South Ossetia (2008). Was named a Hero of the Russian Federation in 2004.

BIOGRAPHY

Palagin was born on March 26, 1968 in Saratov. The Palagin family often had to change their place of living due to the change of his father's job as an oilman. His family soon moved to Ashkhabad, Turkmenia.
In 1985 he was drafted into the Armed Forces of the USSR by the Proletarian District Military Enlistment Committee of Ashkhabad. In 1989, he graduated from the Saratov Higher Military Aviation School of Pilots.
After graduation he served in the aviation helicopter units of the Soviet Army Group in Germany. Since 1993, he served in the 487th Independent Helicopter Regiment stationed in Budyonnovsk.
He took part in combat operations in a number of "hot spots" on the territory of the former USSR, including the liquidation of the Ossetian-Ingush conflict in 1993. He participated in the First Chechen War, in combat operations in Dagestan in August-September 1999, and made several tours of duty to participate in combat operations during the Second Chechen War. By 2004, he had flown 2,512 combat sorties.
By presidential decree of April 6, 2004, Major Palagin was awarded the title of Hero of the Russian Federation for his courage and heroism in the performance of his military duty in the North Caucasus region.
He continued his service in the Russian Army, was deputy commander of the 487th separate helicopter regiment, then a senior flight safety inspector of the 1st class air base in Budyonnovsk. Since 2014 Colonel S.V. Palagin was in the reserve.
He lived in Krasnodar. He was actively engaged in public and patriotic-educational work. He was the deputy chairman of the Krasnodar regional organization of Heroes of the Fatherland, a member of the regional council of the public organization "Battle Brotherhood", a military foreman of the Kuban Cossack Army.
In 2011 he graduated from the Institute of "Friendship of Peoples of the Caucasus" in Stavropol as a psychologist-teacher. Sergey Vyacheslavovich passed away at the age of 52 as a result of chronic illness on November 6, 2020.

S.V.PALAGIN'S HEROICS

In the summer of 2000, several airborne soldiers were wounded in combat in a mountainous region of Chechnya. The enemy fired automatic weapons and several grenade launchers at Palagin's Mi-8 helicopter, which had just arrived. Nevertheless, the coordinated work of the crew made it possible to quickly load the wounded and the commander's skill allowed the heavy vehicle to escape from the fire. All of the wounded were safely transported to the base of the Russian forces.
In June 2002, near the village of Shali, superior insurgent forces drove a group of 16 Russian reconnaissance men into a minefield, and three of the men were seriously wounded by explosions. When Sergei Palagin, commander of the helicopter squadron, arrived at the site of the battle under enemy fire, he discovered that the group was in the woods. The commander decided to go down, breaking the branches of the trees with the fuselage of the helicopter. The vehicle hovered over the abyss for 17 minutes. In the end, the evacuation was completed.
In the summer of 2002 he participated in the rescue of people during the catastrophic flooding in the regions of the North Caucasus. Repeatedly presented to the title of Hero of the Russian Federation.

"BRATISHKA"

Palagin's Mi-8 MTV-2 had its own name "Bratishka", given to it by the soldiers whom the helicopter's experienced crew often rescued in a critical situation. There was an opinion that the helicopter and crew were "conspired" because the machine often came out of combat without much damage, and the crew did not suffer any losses during their service. Because of this, the presence of the Palagin crew on the battlefield or its participation in various operations raised the morale of soldiers and had a generally beneficial effect.
